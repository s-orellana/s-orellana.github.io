Sofia Orellana

Neuroscientist

I combine clinical data, neuroimaging and network science to understand how early experiences and physical health shape the developing brain, from childhood through adulthood.

I recently completed my PhD under Ed Bullmore & Petra Vértes at the University of Cambridge.

Research

In my first project I examined how immuno-metabolic health changes associated with early life stress are linked to differences in brain structure in adulthood. Brain and body are often examined separately, despite ample evidence of their mutual influence. I believe that properly describing these interactions can teach us something about how psychiatric illness is developed and sustained, and how we may effectively treat it.

In my second project I described how different brain regions are synchronised in their structural change across adolescence. Some regions primarily orchestrate sensory and motor processes (seeing, riding a bike), others orchestrate more abstract thought (thinking about others, planning dinner). If we understand how these are jointly changing after puberty, then we can better understand the unique emotional and social changes we observe in adolescents.

Methods & tools

Statistics & modelling

Multivariate models linking brain, body and behaviour, including mediation analyses; graph-theoretic analyses of structural brain networks; structural similarity / morphometric similarity network construction; analyses of coordinated brain maturation across development.

Neuroimaging

Structural MRI and diffusion-weighted imaging. FreeSurfer, FSL and SPM — from raw data through preprocessing, surface reconstruction, manual quality control, and network construction.

Programming & infrastructure

Python, R, MATLAB and Bash. Comfortable building and running analysis pipelines on high-performance computing (SLURM) clusters, and working with large cohorts — including UK Biobank (n ≈ 10,000) and deeply-phenotyped longitudinal developmental samples.

Teaching

Taught MRI preprocessing with FreeSurfer, Bash scripting and HPC job submission to Master's students at the University of Cambridge.
